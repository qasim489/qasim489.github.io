CRTC registration for podcasts and streaming companies draws criticism

Published on October 3, 2023 by Qasim Khan

Dive into the intricate web of Canadian media regulation as the CRTC's new mandates spark debates on freedom of expression, industry uncertainty, and the future of online content creation. ***Article Summary:** The Canadian Radio-television and Telecommunications Commission (CRTC) has announced that online streaming and podcasting services operating in Canada with $10 million or more in annual revenue will have to register with the CRTC by November 28. This move is part of the implementation of the Online Streaming Act (Bill C-11), which requires streaming and online services to support Canadian content. While the CRTC argues that registration is a "very light" burden, critics, including University of Ottawa law professor Michael Geist and Canadian podcaster Jesse Brown, express concerns. Geist sees this move as a signal for potential future regulations, and Brown emphasizes the uncertainty it creates for the industry, particularly smaller players. **Critical Analysis:** From a university political science perspective, this development raises important questions about the balance between regulatory oversight and freedom of expression. While the CRTC insists that registration is not censorship, the requirement for companies to register based on revenue thresholds could be viewed as a form of indirect control over media content. Additionally, the vague definitions and lack of clarity regarding what constitutes "Canadian content" and the obligations of these registered companies introduce ambiguity into the regulatory landscape. This ambiguity might create challenges for companies, especially smaller ones, in understanding their responsibilities and adapting to potential future regulations. Furthermore, the fact that social media users are not required to register raises questions about the consistency and fairness of the regulations, as different platforms are subjected to different rules. Overall, this situation highlights the complexities and debates surrounding media regulation, freedom of expression, and the evolving digital landscape in the context of national identity and cultural preservation.
